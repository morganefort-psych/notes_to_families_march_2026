Talking to Your Child
About Death

This document has been prepared by the school. It draws on validated psychological research to help you find the right words.

School Psychologist · 2025–2026

Why this guide, right now?

What has happened

The mother of two students at our school passed away yesterday. Your children will hear about this, at school, in the hallways, or at home. It is important that you can support them with the right words, before they fill in the gaps themselves with their imagination.

This guide does not ask you to be perfect. It asks you to be honest, calm, and present.

What psychology tells us

Validated by AAP 2024 · UNICEF · Child Mind Institute

1
Talking is better than silence. Silence does not protect children, it isolates them and feeds anxiety. Naming reality helps children move through it.
2
Direct words are kinder than euphemisms. "Gone to sleep" or "we lost her" create confusion and fear. Use the words: died, death, deceased.
"Your role is not to erase the pain,
but to be there — calm and present."

Two ages, two ways of understanding death

🌱 Preschool / Kindergarten · Ages 3–5

Death as something temporary

At this age, children do not yet understand that death is permanent. They may ask "When is mummy coming back?" several times in the same day, without that meaning they did not understand.

What your child may experience:

  • Believing death is reversible, like in cartoons
  • Thinking it is their fault (magical thinking: "I was naughty")
  • Fearing they might "catch" death like an illness
  • Regression: bedwetting, tantrums, nightmares
  • Asking the same questions again and again, this is normal, it is their way of processing

What to say, concrete examples:

"[the student]'s mummy has died. That means her body completely stopped working, her heart, her breathing, everything. She won't be coming back."
If your child asks "But why can't she come back?" → "When someone has died, their body can't work anymore at all. That is how life works."
If your child says "Is it because I was naughty?" → "No, not at all. It is nobody's fault. You had nothing to do with this."

What helps most at this age:

  • Routine, same mealtimes, same bedtime, same rituals
  • Physical closeness, cuddles, holding, nearness
  • Allowing play, play is how young children process emotions
  • Re-reading the same books, repetition is reassuring
  • Short, patient answers to repeated questions

📚 Grade 2 · Ages 7–8

Death as a permanent reality

At this age, children understand that death is permanent and universal. They may ask biological questions ("How do you know when someone is dead?") and existential ones ("Are you going to die too?"). They may also feel guilty or worried about the surviving parent.

What your child may experience:

  • Guilt, "If I had been good, maybe..."
  • Fear of losing the other parent or a loved one
  • Curiosity about biological details, this is healthy, not morbid
  • Intermittent sadness, seeming "normal" then suddenly breaking down
  • Difficulty concentrating at school
  • Wanting to protect adults, hiding sadness so as not to worry them

What to say, concrete examples:

"You may have heard that [the student]'s mummy has died. It's true. It's sad, and it's normal to feel a lot of things or even nothing at all, and that's okay. You can ask me any questions you have."
If your child asks "Are you going to die too?" → "One day, yes, like everyone. But not right now. And you will never be alone, there will always be adults to take care of you."
If your child says "Maybe it was [the student]'s fault" → "No. A parent's death is never a child's fault. Never."

What helps most at this age:

  • Answer factual questions honestly, without over-explaining
  • Validate all emotions: sadness, anger, relief, fear, nothing — all are normal
  • Give them a concrete role if they need it: draw a card, prepare something, action helps
  • Maintain normal activities: sport, friends, outings
  • Don't over-protect them: they need facts, not silence

Common questions from your child, how to respond

Your child says or asks...You can respond...
"How did she die?""Her body stopped working. I don't know all the details, and that's not what matters. What matters is that it happened and it's very sad for [the student] and [the student]'s family."
"Am I going to die too?""Everyone dies one day, but you have so many years ahead of you, and I am here to take care of you."
"Who will look after [the student] / [the student] now?""Her family. There are people, adults in her family who are taking care of her."
"I don't want to go to school""I understand. It's difficult. But school will help you have a normal day with your friends and your teacher, there will be good moments. I'll be here tonight."
"I don't feel anything""That's completely normal. We don't always feel something straight away, and that's okay."
"Why are you crying?""Because it's sad, and crying helps me feel better. You're allowed to cry too."
Silence, seems withdrawnDon't force it. Stay present. Offer a cuddle or activity together, without requiring them to talk.

Words to avoid, and warning signs

❌ Avoid sayingWhy it can cause harm
"She's gone", "we lost her", "she went to sleep"Children may fear sleeping or getting lost. Use the words: died, death, deceased.
"She's in heaven / with the angels"Too abstract for young children. Only use if it is a genuine belief in your family.
"We shouldn't talk about it"Silence isolates and amplifies anxiety. The child thinks the subject is too dangerous to approach.
"You're strong, you'll get through it"Invalidates emotions. The child may feel they must hide their sadness.
"Think about something else"Children need to move through their emotions, not avoid them.
Too many medical or contextual detailsAdapt to the child's age. Disturbing details should be avoided.
⚠ When to seek help — consult a professional if:
Most reactions are normal and temporary. Consult a professional if you notice:
Repeated nightmares or intense fear of sleeping (lasting more than 2–3 weeks)
Persistent refusal to go to school
Comments about not wanting to live or joining the deceased
Lasting regression (bedwetting, mutism, loss of language)
Complete withdrawal from friends and activities
Repeated physical complaints with no medical cause (stomach aches, headaches)

Therapeutic activities to do at home

Research in childhood grief shows that creative expression helps process what words cannot yet contain. (Sesar et al., 2024, Mediterranean Journal of Clinical Psychology)

🎨 For Preschool / Kindergarten (ages 3–5)

  • Draw "[the student]'s family" or "someone we miss"
  • Look at photos of loved ones while talking about them
  • Read together: Goodbye Mog (Judith Kerr) · The Invisible String (Patrice Karst) · The Goodbye Book (Todd Parr)
  • Plant a seed together, a simple way to talk about the cycle of life

📖 For Grade 2 (ages 7–8)

  • Create a "memory box": drawings, photos, small objects that remind you of someone
  • Write a letter to the person who has died, it doesn't need to be sent
  • Read together: The Memory Box (Joanna Rowland) · When Dinosaurs Die (Laurie Krasny Brown)
  • Share your own memories of grandparents or loved ones who have died

What the experts say

These authors have not all written specifically about the death of a parent, but their contributions are directly applicable to this context.

Serge Tisseron
Psychiatrist and psychoanalyst · Family secrets and the unspoken
Silence around a complicated death seeps through: children pick up on the unspoken and build a mistaken logic. Images and drawing are powerful therapeutic mediators for children who cannot yet find words.
Les secrets de famille (PUF)
Christophe André
Psychiatrist · Emotions and mindfulness
A parent who cries while explaining "I'm crying because I'm sad and that's normal" teaches their child that difficult emotions can be survived. Simple mindfulness practices (breathing, grounding) help children stabilise.
Hélène Romano
Clinical psychologist, trauma specialist · France
The warning sign is not tears but sudden changes in behaviour. Expressing your own sadness is therapeutic for children, not dangerous. Talk about death naturally from the earliest age, even with babies.
David J. Schonfeld
Paediatrician, USC / NCSCB · Childhood grief in schools
The world's leading expert on school-based childhood bereavement. Teachers do not need to become therapists: their role is to show empathy, name reality, and initiate the conversation rather than wait for the child to speak first.
The Grieving Student (with Marcia Quackenbush) · grievingstudents.org
Phyllis Silverman
Researcher, Harvard · Continuing bonds
Children maintain an active inner relationship with their deceased parent, and this is healthy. Based on the Harvard Child Bereavement Study, they need a vocabulary to name what they feel, and adults who refuse to talk about death deprive them of the tools to grieve.
Never Too Young to Know: Death in Children's Lives
J. William Worden
Clinical psychologist, Harvard · Tasks of mourning
Grief is not a passive series of stages but a set of active tasks. A child may seem to have "moved on" then revisit grief at each new developmental stage — this is normal, not a sign of regression.
Children and Grief: When a Parent Dies

Women researchers · Grief studies

Elisabeth Kübler-Ross
Psychiatrist · Swiss-American (1926–2004) · Pioneer of thanatology
Creator of the "five stages of grief" model, still the global reference in death and dying. She devoted a major part of her career to dying children and their families, insisting on their right to truth and their own voice about death. Time magazine named her one of the "100 most important thinkers of the 20th century".
On Death and Dying (1969) · On Grief and Grieving (2005, with David Kessler)
Marie-Frédérique Bacqué
Professor of psychopathology · University of Strasbourg · France
President of the French Thanatology Society, co-founder of the Vivre son Deuil association, and author of over 100 scientific articles on grief and loss. For 30 years she has worked with bereaved children and families. She emphasises that collective rituals are essential for children: naming the deceased, giving them a place, allowing them to say goodbye.
Apprivoiser la mort (Odile Jacob) · Le deuil à vivre (Odile Jacob)
Nathalie Parent
Psychologist, author and speaker · Quebec, Canada
Specialist in supporting grieving children for over 20 years. She emphasises not "doing it for" the child, but walking alongside them at their own pace. Grief in children resurfaces at each developmental stage — this is not relapse, it is their natural way of integrating loss.
Le deuil d'Olivia (Saint-Jean Éditeur) · Accompanying a Child Through Grief (Educatout)

🌿 Seasons for Growth (MacKillop, 2023) · A guide to self-care and wellbeing during times of uncertainty ↗

Seasons for Growth is an international grief and life-transition support programme used in Australia, Ireland and the United Kingdom. Created by the MacKillop Foundation, it is designed for children, adolescents and adults. Its starting point is simple: change and loss are part of life, and we can learn to navigate them with the right tools. The wellbeing guide, freely downloadable, is particularly useful for parents supporting children through bereavement while going through a difficult time themselves.

  • Listen to your own needs · Offer kindness to yourself and others
  • Maintain a healthy routine (diet, physical activity, sleep)
  • Focus on what you can control, not what you cannot
  • Stay socially connected · Seek support when you need it
  • Build hope: note 3 things you are grateful for each day
  • Reflect on past difficulties and what helped you through them
"You can't pour from an empty cup. Look after yourself so you are better able to help the children in your care." Contact: seasons@mackillop.org.au · mackillop.org.au/seasons-for-growth

What your children hear at home

Children learn important social norms by listening to adult conversations, and they do this far more than we realise. When a child picks up fragments of conversations about a complicated death, they fill in the gaps with their imagination. If what they hear seems negative or confusing, distrust grows and rumour spreads quickly.

Comments made at home can arrive at school the next day in this form:

  • "My mum said it's because she was taking drugs"
  • "My dad says it's someone's fault"
  • "She was a bad mother, that's why"
  • "I heard the school called the police on her"

These comments, even unintentional ones, can deeply hurt [the student] when repeated in class. You have a key role to play at home.

💬 What you can say to your child

"You may have heard things about this. What matters is that [the student] is hurting right now and needs their classmates. The rest is for the adults to deal with."
"Adult conversations can be complicated. Even grown-ups don't always know the full story. What we do know is that [the student] needs you as a friend right now."

⭐ The golden rule

Your children will hear things. The question is whether what they hear comes from a kind, considered conversation with you, or from fragments overheard without context.

💙
You don't need to have all the answers. What your child needs is to know they can talk to you, that you are not falling apart, and that life continues.
Your calm presence is the most powerful therapeutic tool you have.
